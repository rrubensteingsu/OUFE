Orangetown United for Full Employment

Excel Assignment No. 1 — Time Value of Money & Program Financial Feasibility

Situation

Orangetown recently suffered major layoffs by its primary employer — a manufacturer of heating systems. You are Executive Director of Orangetown United for Full Employment (OUFE), a nonprofit providing job training and placement services.

You want to launch a new skills training program. The board will only support it if the program is financially self-sufficient over the next six years — meaning total revenues must cover total costs when both are expressed in today’s dollars.

Program Structure

Each cohort consists of up to 35 workers who receive six months of training and earn a certificate. New cohorts start every six months. With 500+ workers laid off, OUFE will run the program for six full years at maximum enrollment — 12 cohorts total.

Why Time Value of Money?

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future because today’s dollar can be invested. When cash flows occur at different points in time, we discount future amounts back to today using a rate that reflects the opportunity cost of money — here, 8%.

Present Value of a Single Lump Sum

For a one-time payment of FV occurring n years from today:

PV = FV / (1 + r)^n Excel: =PV(r, n, 0, -FV) or =FV / (1+r)^n

Use the annual rate when n is in years. A Year-0 cash flow needs no discounting (n = 0).

Present Value of an Ordinary Annuity

When the same payment PMT occurs at the end of each period for n periods:

PV = PMT × [1 − (1 + r)^(-n)] / r Excel: =PV(rate, nper, -pmt) ← note the negative sign

In Excel, PV returns a negative when you enter positive PMT; negate the PMT argument or the result.

Annuity Due (Beginning-of-Period Payments)

Payments at the start of each period. Multiply the ordinary annuity PV by (1 + r), or use Excel’s optional type argument:

Excel: =PV(rate, nper, -pmt, 0, 1) ← the final "1" = annuity due

The insurance premium is paid before each year begins — this is an annuity due.

Matching Rate to Period

The rate in a PV formula must match the length of one payment period. If payments occur monthly, you need a monthly rate. If they occur every six months, you need a six-month rate. If annually, you use the annual rate directly.

To convert an annual rate to a shorter period: divide by the number of those periods in a year. Then set nper to the total number of those periods over the full life of the cash flow stream.

monthly rate = annual rate / 12, nper = total months quarterly rate = annual rate / 4, nper = total quarters semi-annual rate = annual rate / 2, nper = total 6-mo periods

A common mistake: aggregating monthly payments into an annual total and discounting once per year. This understates the PV of costs because it ignores that each monthly payment is made earlier than year-end and therefore has a higher PV than a single year-end lump sum.

Net Present Value

NPV = ∑ PV(revenues) − ∑ PV(costs)

NPV > 0 means a surplus — the program pays for itself. NPV < 0 means a deficit — additional funds or cost cuts are needed.

PV Function Syntax

=PV(rate, nper, pmt, [fv], [type]) — rate: the periodic discount rate (must match the payment period); nper: total number of payment periods; pmt: payment per period (enter as negative for outflows); fv: optional lump sum at end (default 0); type: 0 = payment at end of period (default), 1 = payment at beginning. Excel returns a positive PV when pmt is entered as negative.

Rate and Period Must Match

The most important decision for each cash flow is: what is one period? The answer determines both the rate you use and the nper you count. If one period is a month, use a monthly rate and count months. If one period is six months, use a six-month rate and count six-month intervals. Dividing the annual rate by the number of periods per year gives you the periodic rate. Store the annual rate in one cell and derive the others from it — then changing 8% to 6% recalculates everything automatically.

Lump Sum PV

Two equivalent approaches: =FV/(1+rate)^n or =PV(rate, n, 0, -FV). For Year-0 items their PV equals the face value — no discounting needed. For any lump sum at year n, use the annual rate with n equal to the number of years until payment.

Annuity Due (Beginning-of-Period Payments)

By default, Excel's PV function assumes payments at the end of each period (ordinary annuity). When payments are made at the start of each period, use the optional 5th argument: =PV(rate, nper, -pmt, 0, 1). The 1 flags it as an annuity due. Look carefully at the insurance premium — when is it paid relative to the year?

Monthly vs. Annual Treatment of Recurring Costs

When a cost recurs every month, you have a choice: treat it as a monthly annuity (using a monthly rate and counting months), or aggregate it into an annual total and treat it as a single year-end payment. These give different answers. Think carefully about which better reflects reality — is the cash actually paid monthly, or at year-end?

DOL Payments — Timing Matters

The DOL grant is paid at each cohort's graduation. Think about how frequently cohorts graduate and use that payment frequency to define your period. The payment per period is the per-graduate amount times the cohort size.
